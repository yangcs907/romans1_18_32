God's Wrath on Unrighteousness

 Gospel Glimpses

The Gospel Makes us "Natural"

Paul highlights how the inward bent of sin creates such dysfunction and disorder that we defy the sovereign lordship of Christ, pursuing abnormal and unnatural relations as if they were natural and normal. In Romans 1:26-27, Paul comments on the sinfulness of homosexuality and how it deviates from the "natural" and is instead "contrary to nature" and "shameless". Elsewhere Paul writes that before salvation we "lived in the passions of our flesh, carrying out the desires of the body and the mind" (Ephesians 2:3) The corollary to this truth is that after salvation, even as we become more and more conformed to the image of Christ, we are also becoming more and more our true selves. As the fruit of the Spirit increases in us, God restores order within us. Our minds are renewed (Romans 12:2) . We learn to do the natural thing spiritually and the spiritual thing naturally.

 Whole-Bible Connections

Idolatry

Paul shows us that the fundamental problem of human existence is the worship of false gods. Idolatry is the root of sin. He outlines the problem with humanity in this way: "Claiming to be wise, they became fools, and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things..." (Romans 1:22-23). He continues by saying, "They exchanged the truth about God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator, who is blessed forever!" (Romans 1:25). Humans are never not worshiping. The story of Israel in the Old Testament shows God's people continually struggling to trust the Lord and lapsing again into idolatry.

Divine Sovreignty

Consider these veres in Romans 1
"Therefore God gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity" (v.24)
"For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions." (v.26)
"For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions." (v.26)
God is "giving up" unrepentant sinners to their sin. In allowing sin to persist and harden a person's heart, God maintains his sovereignty in a way that does not negate human responsibility. The clearest example is in the way God dealt with Pharoah in the book of Exodus. We read that Pharoah harded his heart (Exodus 8:15,32), but we also read that God hardened Pharoah's heart (Exodus 4:21, 7:3, 9:12). So which is it? In the mystery of God's sovereignty and human responsibility, we learn that the answer is "both". Sinners are responsible for their hard hearts, yet God is sovereign over human activity.

 Theological Soundings

General Revelation

Romans 1:19-20 sounds very similar to Psalm 19:1. The theological concept here is what is commonly called the doctrine of "general revelation". Drawn from the Scriptures, this idea is that God reveals himself in the created world. This does not claim that everything we need to know about God and his gospel in Christ is intelligible from the natural world. Gospel knowledge necessitates what is called "special revelation", which refers to God's Word in the flesh (Jesus) and God's Word written down (Bible). With general revelation, it does mean that there is enough of God's glory revealed in creation to leave sinners with no excuse for their unbelief and idolatry (Romans 1:20).
